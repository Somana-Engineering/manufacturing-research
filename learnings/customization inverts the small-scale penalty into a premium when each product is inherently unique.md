# customization inverts the small-scale penalty into a premium when each product is inherently unique

Conventional manufacturing economics assumes small batches are a penalty — [[setup cost and optimal batch size are mathematically linked through EOQ so reducing changeover time enables smaller batches and leaner inventory|EOQ dictates larger batches to amortize setup costs]], and [[factory costs divide into fixed variable and semi-variable categories that determine operating leverage|operating leverage punishes underutilization]]. But across medical devices, cosmetics, and fashion, small scale is not a limitation to overcome — it IS the value proposition.

The evidence is consistent across categories. Patient-specific orthopedic implants (restor3d, $192M raised) are manufactured from individual CT scans — mass production is literally impossible. Custom nail polish (Blank Beauty, 4,000 sq ft micro-factory) is created in under 2 minutes by AI + robotic dosing. Ministry of Supply knits custom blazers in-store in 90 minutes at a 21% price premium ($345 vs $285 standard). SmartSKN deploys "skincare robots" formulating from 25,000+ ingredient combinations with 98% diagnostic accuracy.

The margins compensate for throughput: cosmetics achieves 70-85% gross margins at COGS of $4.50-$6.50/unit. Medical devices command even higher margins behind regulatory certification moats. Custom fashion achieves premiums specifically because the product is unique.

This challenges the assumption embedded in [[Wright's Law predicts manufacturing costs decline by a fixed percentage with each cumulative production doubling|Wright's Law]] — that cost reduction requires cumulative volume. When AI handles design generation, formulation, and production routing at near-zero marginal cost per variant, the cost of variety collapses. [[AI shrinks the fixed cost base making operating leverage less punishing for underutilization and less rewarding for high utilization|AI shrinks the fixed cost of variety management]] — design, quoting, quality systems — making batch-of-one economically rational.

For Somana, this means the mini factory's small scale IS a strategic feature. The product portfolio research confirms: IoT environmental sensors in custom 3D-printed enclosures ($50–$120, 50–70% margins), STEM electronics kits with custom project enclosures ($60–$95, 55–70% margins), and even 3D-printed eyewear frames ($175–$800, 60–80% margins where material costs are $5–$20). Batch.Works in London has manufactured over 500,000 3D-printed parts across 160 clients as "Circular Manufacturing as a Service," reaching £3.5M valuation. The [[contract box-build assembly for 10 to 500 units is massively underserved because tooling costs and minimum order quantities block hardware startups|contract box-build market]] for 10–500 units is similarly premised on inherent uniqueness — every hardware startup's product is different.
